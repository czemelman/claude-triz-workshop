_The brief describes a two-axis tension: the symmetric NFC chip path (NTAG424 DNA) gives the best consumer UX and lowest cost but requires constructed continuity via Shamir escrow — a defensible but operator-dependent guarantee. The asymmetric chip path (Arx HaLo) gives inherent continuity with on-die ECDSA but requires an app install, costs 3–8x more, and introduces single-supplier risk. Options D–H attempt to dissolve the contradiction by inserting a backend intermediary (Nitro Enclave, HKDF-derived ECDSA attestation) that produces asymmetric proofs from symmetric chips — but this reintroduces a trusted-operator dependency in a different form, which is precisely what the continuity wedge claims to eliminate. Confidence is medium rather than high because the brief itself contains three plausible sub-contradictions (UX vs continuity, continuity-quality vs solo-dev complexity, cost vs guarantee strength) and the primary framing — UX vs continuity guarantee strength — is the one that most directly drives the chip architecture decision, but a secondary framing around solo-dev complexity vs system trustworthiness is also valid and could route to different matrix parameters._

## Candidate Solutions

### Candidate: Dual-plane issuance with latent shard anti-weight

Separate the continuity guarantee from the consumer UX by provisioning two independent planes at chip-bonding time: a primary UX plane (NTAG424 DNA, sub-€1, zero-friction tap) and an orthogonal proof plane (an on-chain EIP-712 commitment recorded in the SignerRegistry on Base L2 at issuance, encoding the CMAC-derived binding proof, the Shamir-shard root commitment hash, and a pointer to an open-source offline verifier pinned to IPFS and Arweave). The operator-dependency weakness of the symmetric chip is counterweighted by making the proof plane latently operator-independent: after a hypothetical vendor shutdown, any M-of-N Shamir shard holder can reconstruct the derivation root and run the open-source verifier against the chip CMAC output without touching live infrastructure. The two planes improve independently — frictionless consumer UX does not degrade the proof plane's survivability, and the proof plane's robustness does not add cost or friction to the consumer tap.

- **Novelty:** high | **Effort:** medium
- **Principles applied:** P_ANOTHER_DIMENSION, P_ANTI_WEIGHT

**Implementation sketch.**

At chip-bonding time, the provisioning service runs in two parallel tracks: Track A generates the NTAG424 DNA CMAC key set via HKDF derivation from the KMS root and writes it to the chip; Track B constructs an EIP-712 typed struct containing the HKDF derivation commitment (a one-way hash of the derivation path and the chip's unique identifier), the Shamir-shard root commitment hash (not the shards themselves), and a content-addressed URL pointing to the open-source offline verifier published to IPFS and mirrored on Arweave. Track B then calls the SignerRegistry contract on Base L2 to record this struct, producing a permanent on-chain anchor. The on-chain record is the anti-weight: it survives vendor shutdown because Base L2 state is not under the operator's control. The open-source verifier (a small CLI or web app) needs no network calls against operator infrastructure — it takes the chip's CMAC output, the reconstructed HKDF root (from the M-of-N shards), and verifies the derivation locally. The consumer-facing flow is entirely in Track A: NFC tap, CMAC verification against the live backend, EIP-712 event emitted — no change to the consumer experience. The continuity marketing claim is now dual-path: normal-operations path uses the live KMS backend; post-shutdown path uses shard reconstruction plus the pinned verifier. The Shamir shards are distributed to escrow parties at provisioning time under the existing YubiHSM 2 / M-of-N architecture already settled in the constraints. A quarterly smoke-test procedure (run the offline verifier against a sample chip using only the shards and the on-chain record, with KMS intentionally offline) provides auditable evidence that the anti-weight path is functional, which strengthens the continuity marketing claim from rhetorical to demonstrably tested.

**Interpretations contributing:**
- _triz-ai-extended_ on **Another dimension** (triz_ai_50x50): Stop treating the continuity guarantee as a single flat property that a chip either has or lacks, and instead layer it across two distinct planes: a primary UX plane (the NTAG424 DNA tap, cheap, frictionless, no app) and an orthogonal proof plane (an on-chain EIP-712 attestation anchored at chip-provisioning time on Base L2, signed by the HKDF-derived key set, and recorded in the SignerRegistry). The consumer interaction lives entirely in the UX plane; the continuity claim lives in the proof plane. A verifier who wants to audit continuity after a hypothetical vendor shutdown reads the proof plane directly from the chain — no operator is needed for that read. The two planes are provisioned together at chip issuance but operate independently at verification time, so improving UX frictionlessness in the UX plane does not degrade the proof plane's independence.
- _triz-ai-extended_ on **Anti-weight / Counterweight** (triz_ai_50x50): Counteract the reliability weakness of constructed continuity (KMS-escrow dependency) by attaching a parallel, self-sustaining continuity anchor that does not require the operator to remain alive. Concretely, at chip-bonding time, write an irrevocable on-chain commitment to Base L2 via the SignerRegistry that encodes the chip's CMAC-derived binding proof, the Shamir-shard root commitment hash, and a pointer to an open-source offline verifier pinned to IPFS and Arweave. This on-chain record becomes the anti-weight: even if AWS KMS, the backend, and the company vanish, any holder of the original M-of-N Shamir shards can reconstruct the derivation root and run the open-source verifier against the chip's CMAC output without touching any live operator infrastructure. The constructed continuity claim is now backed by two independent weight-bearing mechanisms — the live KMS path for day-to-day operations and the shard-reconstruction path for post-shutdown verification — so the rhetorical weakness of 'operator-dependent' is neutralised without replacing the symmetric chip.

**Critique.**

- **Severity:** moderate
- **Recommendation:** pursue with refinement; the secondary contradictions are addressable — formalise the shard escrow agreement as a legally binding arrangement with defined succession, design the offline verifier to be dependency-minimal and source-reproducible from a fixed dependency snapshot, and budget for Arweave permanent storage at provisioning time. The candidate is otherwise well-matched to the solo-developer constraint and the sub-€1 chip requirement.
- **Secondary contradictions:**
  - operator-independent post-shutdown verifiability via Shamir shard reconstruction ⇄ Shamir shard custody lifecycle complexity for a solo developer (moderate)
  - permanence and verifiability of the on-chain proof plane (IPFS/Arweave-pinned verifier) ⇄ long-term software maintainability of the pinned offline verifier (moderate)
  - independence of the proof plane from the live KMS backend ⇄ strength of the provable chip-binding that the proof plane provides (moderate)
- **Risks:**
  - Shard holders failing to maintain custody over a 10–30 year bottle lifetime: if fewer than M-of-N shard holders remain reachable or functional, the offline verification path is permanently unavailable. The quarterly smoke-test procedure proposed in the implementation sketch must be formalised in a legal escrow agreement with teeth, not a voluntary protocol.
  - IPFS content addressing does not guarantee persistence: IPFS pins can be garbage-collected if the pinning node goes down and no other node replicates the CID. The Arweave mirror is a meaningful backstop but adds a one-time storage cost. The candidate must budget for permanent Arweave storage of the verifier at issuance time.
  - Base L2 network continuity: the on-chain proof plane depends on the Base L2 network remaining readable. If Base L2 is deprecated or sunsets in 15+ years, the SignerRegistry records may become inaccessible without a migration plan. This is a low-probability risk over short horizons but meaningful over the cellar-lifetime horizon.
  - The 'constructed continuity' weakness is not fully resolved: the candidate produces a stronger-than-rhetorical guarantee but the guarantee is still conditional on shard holder cooperation, not inherent in the chip hardware. Premium wine buyers or high-value secondary-market participants may find this distinction marketing-material relevant.
  - Quarterly smoke-test as an operational burden: a solo developer must execute and record the smoke-test on schedule. Missing a quarter creates a gap in the 'auditable evidence' chain. No automated enforcement of this procedure is described.

### Candidate: Tier-split composite with scaled counterweight per SKU

Accept that no single chip type is optimal across all price points and build a composite per-SKU architecture: NTAG424 DNA on all volume-tier bottles (sub-€1, constructed continuity backed by the on-chain IPFS anti-weight anchor), and an additional Arx HaLo asymmetric chip embedded in the tamper-evident capsule seal on premium-tier bottles (inherent on-die ECDSA continuity, tolerating the higher chip cost where margin supports it). The anti-weight mechanism is SKU-proportional: volume SKUs get the shard-reconstruction plus IPFS verifier anchor as their counterforce against KMS dependency; premium SKUs get that anchor plus the on-die ECDSA key as a second, hardware-level counterweight, meaning the continuity guarantee at the premium tier is doubly anti-weighted against operator failure. The SignerRegistry on Base L2 registers both chip types under the same bottle identity token so verification calls are uniform regardless of which tier is being checked.

- **Novelty:** medium | **Effort:** medium
- **Principles applied:** P_ANTI_WEIGHT, P_COMPOSITE_MATERIALS

**Implementation sketch.**

The provisioning pipeline branches on SKU tier at the start of the chip-bonding workflow. For volume SKUs, only the NTAG424 DNA path runs: HKDF-derived CMAC keys are written to the chip, and the on-chain anti-weight anchor (shard root commitment hash plus IPFS verifier pointer) is recorded in the SignerRegistry exactly as in Candidate 1. For premium SKUs, a second stage runs: an Arx HaLo chip is bonded to the capsule seal, its public key is registered in the SignerRegistry alongside the NTAG424 DNA binding, and an on-die ECDSA attestation signed at bonding time is recorded on-chain. The HaLo chip signs a challenge at every verification for premium buyers who use the HaLo-aware path (e.g. via an NFC-capable browser extension or a future lightweight PWA), but the NTAG424 tap path remains available on premium bottles for consumers who prefer frictionless interaction — the premium label simply carries both chips. The SignerRegistry contract stores a chip-tier flag per bottle token so verifiers know which continuity evidence to expect. Premium buyers can therefore be shown a stronger continuity proof in the UI while volume buyers see the standard constructed-continuity proof. The capsule-seal form factor for the HaLo on premium bottles mitigates the solo-developer operational complexity concern: the NTAG424 provisioning pipeline remains unchanged for the dominant volume tier; the HaLo provisioning is an additive step only for the premium production run. Cost structure: volume tier stays sub-€1 per chip; premium tier absorbs the 3–8x HaLo cost, which is acceptable at premium price points.

- **Severity:** severe
- **Recommendation:** refine before pursuing; the severe secondary contradiction (solo-developer provisioning complexity for a dual chip-type pipeline) needs explicit mitigation — define a mandatory post-provisioning HaLo challenge-and-registration-verification step, validate dual-inlay sourcing with at least one label manufacturer before committing, and decide whether to accept the re-introduced Arx single-supplier risk at the premium tier or substitute an equivalent asymmetric chip with a second qualified source.
- **Secondary contradictions:**
  - continuity guarantee strength at the premium tier (on-die ECDSA as second anti-weight) ⇄ solo-developer provisioning and supply-chain operational complexity (severe)
  - marketing differentiation between premium and volume tier (stronger proof on premium) ⇄ verifier code-path uniformity and consumer experience consistency (moderate)
  - single-supplier dependency mitigation (NTAG424 as primary, HaLo as secondary on premium) ⇄ Arx HaLo supplier dependency risk for the premium-tier guarantee (moderate)
- **Risks:**
  - Silent HaLo provisioning failures on premium bottles: if the HaLo chip bonding or on-chain key registration step fails partway through a production run, affected premium bottles will have the NTAG424 tap path working normally but the HaLo continuity path broken. Without a mandatory post-provisioning verification step that challenges the HaLo and checks the on-chain registration, these failures will not be detected until an auditor attempts the premium-tier proof path.
  - Dual-inlay label sourcing risk: sourcing a label substrate that incorporates both NTAG424 DNA and Arx HaLo antennas in a single inlay from a manufacturer who supports both chip types is a supply-chain dependency that must be validated before the design is committed to. If no label manufacturer supports this combination in volume, the design requires separate label substrates for premium and volume tiers, doubling label SKU management.
  - Arx HaLo long-term supply continuity: the premium-tier's hardware guarantee depends on HaLo chips remaining available for re-provisioning of future production runs. If Arx discontinues or is acquired, future production cannot maintain the premium-tier hardware differentiation.
  - Cost structure risk at the premium tier: the implementation sketch says the 3–8x HaLo cost premium 'is acceptable at premium price points' without specifying the premium bottle's margin. If the HaLo chip costs €3–8 and NTAG424 is sub-€1, the chip delta is €2–7 per premium unit. Whether this is absorbed in margin requires explicit validation with the client's pricing model.
  - Two-tier on-chain registration logic in the SignerRegistry contract must be correct and audited: a bug in the chip-tier flag or the dual-registration path could allow a counterfeiter to register a volume-tier bottle as a premium-tier bottle on-chain, falsely elevating its apparent continuity guarantee.

### Candidate: Uniform NTAG424 base with composite continuity anchors

Keep the chip architecture entirely uniform and low-cost (NTAG424 DNA on every SKU, sub-€1) and instead build a composite continuity-anchor strategy from the two operator-independent paths that the software layer can provide: an on-chain shard-root commitment anti-weight (the primary counterforce against KMS failure) combined with a per-SKU-tier secondary anchor that varies in strength — volume SKUs get the IPFS-pinned open-source verifier reference alone, while premium SKUs additionally get a Chainlink-attested or ENS-signed continuity certificate that provides an auditable third-party endorsement of the Shamir escrow configuration. No second chip is required; the composite is entirely in the trust-anchor layer. This is the lowest-effort candidate and trades off the highest continuity guarantee strength for operational simplicity appropriate to a solo developer.

- **Novelty:** medium | **Effort:** low
- **Principles applied:** P_ANTI_WEIGHT, P_CHEAP_SHORT_LIVING, P_COMPOSITE_MATERIALS

**Implementation sketch.**

The chip provisioning pipeline remains exactly as currently settled — NTAG424 DNA with HKDF-derived CMAC keys, KMS on AWS eu-central-1 with BYOK and YubiHSM 2 Shamir M-of-N. The new work is entirely in the on-chain anchor layer. At issuance, the existing SignerRegistry call is extended to write a multi-anchor struct: the HKDF derivation commitment hash, the Shamir shard-root commitment hash, and a content-addressed pointer to the open-source offline verifier (IPFS primary, Arweave mirror). This is the base anti-weight present on all SKUs. For premium SKUs, a second on-chain call registers a continuity certificate signed by a designated third party (an escrow trustee, a legal notary service with ENS identity, or a Chainlink oracle attesting to the Shamir escrow configuration at issuance time). This second anchor adds an external witness to the continuity claim, making the guarantee composite: even if the operator's own attestation is questioned, the third-party witness is an independent signal. The open-source verifier pinned to IPFS is the 'cheap short-living equivalent' in the software sense — it is a small, dependency-minimal tool that a shard holder can run long after the operator's own verification service has shut down, without maintaining any server infrastructure. A smoke-test protocol (quarterly, using only the offline verifier plus shards, with KMS offline) generates a test transaction on Base L2 as auditable evidence that the offline path remains functional. Total new implementation work for a solo developer is approximately one week: extend the SignerRegistry contract call at provisioning time, write and pin the offline verifier, and draft the third-party attestation procedure for premium SKUs. No hardware change, no dual-inlay supply chain work.
